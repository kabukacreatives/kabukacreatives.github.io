Subtopic 2.1
The Burn Test: How to Know in 10 Seconds if Your Silk Is Real
Real silk burns like hair — it self-extinguishes and smells like burnt protein. Synthetic "silk" melts into a plastic bead and smells chemical. Most people have never tested their accessories.
Educational

One 10-second test tells you if the silk you paid for is actually silk.

Hook
Pull a thread from the seam of your silk bag. What happens next tells you everything.
Insight
Silk is a protein fibre — like hair. When burned, it extinguishes itself, crumbles to a soft ash, and smells faintly of burnt hair or feathers. Polyester and synthetic "silk" melts into a hard plastic bead, stays lit, and smells chemical. India's Silk Mark Organisation certifies fewer than 2% of silk products sold online as genuine. That means 98% of what is labelled silk online is, at best, a blend — at worst, pure polyester. The test takes 10 seconds. Most people never do it.
3 Do This Today Steps
1
Pull a single thread from an inside seam of any bag or fabric labelled silk. Hold it with tweezers and bring a lighter to it carefully.
2
Real silk: self-extinguishes, crumbles, smells like hair. Synthetic: melts, keeps burning, smells plastic. If you're not sure, it's probably synthetic.
3
Before ordering any silk accessory online, ask the seller for this test on a sample. A genuine silk seller will welcome the question. A synthetic seller will deflect it.

Subtopic 2.3
Why Synthetic Bags Make Expensive Sarees Look Cheap Even When Nothing Else Goes Wrong
Genuine silk has a particular way of catching light — dimensional and warm. Synthetic "silk" has a flat, cool sheen that photographs completely differently and reads as cheap next to real fabric.
Educational

Real silk and synthetic silk don't just feel different. They photograph completely differently.

Hook
The camera sees what your eye misses. Synthetic "silk" gives it all away.
Insight
Genuine silk is a triangular-cross-section fibre that reflects light at multiple angles simultaneously — creating the warm, dimensional sheen that makes silk instantly recognisable. Synthetic fibres are round in cross-section — they reflect light uniformly, producing a flat, cool, plastic-looking sheen. Next to a genuine handwoven saree, a synthetic clutch reads as cheap even if it looks fine in isolation. The contrast is especially visible in photographs, which is where your event look lives permanently.
3 Do This Today Steps
1
Photograph your current bag next to your saree in natural light. Look at the way each fabric catches the light. Does the bag sheen match the fabric sheen, or does one look flat while the other glows?
2
Before ordering online, ask the seller for a photo of the fabric in direct sunlight — not studio lighting. Studio light hides synthetic sheen. Sunlight reveals it.
3
DM us your saree photo. We'll show you the Kanchi or Banarasi silk option that matches your fabric's specific light behaviour.

Silk gifting is one of the oldest practices in Indian culture — auspicious, meaningful, generational. But when the "silk" being gifted is power-loom polyester-blend, the meaning disappears with the product. The ritual survives. The substance has been replaced.
Educational

Silk gifting is a tradition thousands of years old. Most modern silk gifts will be landfill in 15 years. Here's the gap.

Hook
You gave her silk. But will it still be silk in 20 years?
Insight
Silk gifting in South Asian culture carries specific cultural weight — silk is auspicious, silk represents respect, silk given at weddings and significant occasions is understood to be a lasting gift. The tradition is ancient and meaningful. But the tradition's meaning rests on an assumption that the silk itself is genuine and durable. When the "silk" being gifted is a blend, a synthetic, or a power-loom product with a decade of useful life, the cultural ritual continues — but the substance that gave it meaning has been quietly removed. Gifting genuine handloom silk restores the meaning alongside the tradition.

Subtopic 7.3
How to Spot Heirloom-Quality Silk Before You Buy: 4 Physical Tests Anyone Can Do
Most buyers never physically test silk before buying — they rely entirely on seller claims. Four physical tests, each taking under a minute, reveal the silk's quality, construction, and longevity potential with no equipment required.
Educational

4 physical tests that tell you whether the silk you're buying will last 10 years or 100.

Hook
Heirloom-quality silk passes 4 physical tests. Here's how to do each one before you buy.
Insight
Genuine handloom silk has physical properties that synthetic and power-loom products cannot replicate — and those properties are testable without equipment. The ring test (real silk passes through a ring smoothly; synthetic catches). The scrunch test (silk returns to shape; synthetic holds creases). The weight test (genuine silk is heavier than it looks due to dense weave). The burn test (protein fibre, self-extinguishing). Each test takes under a minute. Together they constitute a complete quality verification that takes 4 minutes total and can be done anywhere you can handle the fabric.
3 Do This Today Steps
1
Find a ring — any ring — and try passing a corner of your silk piece through it. Genuine silk flows through. Synthetic snags. This one test alone filters most fakes.
2
Scrunch a corner of the silk in your fist for 10 seconds. Release. Real silk recovers smoothly. Synthetic holds creases.
3
For future purchases: ask the seller to demonstrate the ring test before you buy. A genuine silk seller will do it immediately. A synthetic seller will offer to "check" and change the subject.
